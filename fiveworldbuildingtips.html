5 Tips for Better World-Building!

We've all been there, starting a project for the very first time with no clue where to start. When I was a baby author, I could TOTALLY relate to this. Thankfully, I've gotten the strategy down a little bit, but if I had to start all over again, here are my TOP 5 world-building tips for anyone who wants to better their worldbuilding!

1. Remember the process is FLUID

I know how tempting it is to feel like a piece of your story is set in stone the moment you write it down. However this can actually be a detrimental mindset. As you are working through your story, you should absolutely be referring back to details you noted originally. However, If you find that something you already noted is no longer serving your process, it is OKAY to change it. There have been numerous times throughout my journey where I have been so confident in a point that I made in my lore or history or whatever, only to realize down the line that it doesnt make sense with how the story actually played out. Let your worldbuilding evolve with you and your storytelling!

2. Take inspiration from the real world

Maybe you've read a story and fallen in love with a leader of a Faerie court, or maybe your dungeon master dropped some crazy lore about an eldritch monster lurking in a forbidden cave. Though altered to cater to todays popular media, many of your favorite stories take inspiration from existing myths and legends of our world. The elves and faeries popular in many modern fantasy stories can actually be attributed to ancient Norse and Celtic folklore. Many myths about giants or centaurs can be attributed to the myths of the Ancient Greeks. If you are stuck in creating intricate myths and legends, consider taking inspiration from of the myths from our own world!

3. Make your characters interesting

We've all heard of flat, round, static and dynamic characters. These designations exist for a reason. You can give your characters layers in multiple ways. Try allowing them to have unique interests or talents like art or music. Real people have fears, hopes and dreams, and your characters should too. Think about what makes your characters tick, what they are most afraid of and what they are most excited about. Let them experience complex emotions and explore how they react to difficult situations!

4. Let your scenery speak

How you introduce or describe the physical setting of a scene can have a HUGE effect on the feeling you are trying to convey. Imagine trying to write a spooky story, but all the lights are working just fine, the telephone lines are still intact and it’s a bright summer afternoon - not as spooky as a dingy basement, right? It might seem insignificant, but things such as lighting and the way dust settles in a room can make all the difference when trying to evoke certain feelings from your audience.

5. Read up on world-building skills

The only wrong way to do world-building is to not do it at all. There are many different and great resources on the practice of worldbuilding that offer many tips on how to build your skills. You should try to check out as many worldbuilding resources as possible. If you need a good resource to start, consider reading “Creating a Fictional World: The Beginner’s Guide to World-Building.” It’s a great resource to learn about the basics AND how to incorporate that work into your writing (though I might be a little biased)
